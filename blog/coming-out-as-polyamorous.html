Coming Out As Polyamorous

As a person who has navigated the complexities of polyamory and ethical non-monogamy, I've learned that coming out as polyamorous can be a daunting experience. Many people in the LGBTQ+ community have shared their stories of coming out to family and friends, but what about those who identify as polyamorous? In this post, we'll explore some practical tips and real-life scenarios to help you navigate the process of coming out as polyamorous.

Choosing When and Where to Come Out

When deciding when and where to come out as polyamorous, it's essential to consider your comfort level and safety. Polyamory can be a sensitive topic for some people, and you may encounter reactions ranging from surprise to judgment. Take time to think about the potential outcomes and choose situations where you feel confident and supported.

For instance, I came out as polyamorous to my closest friends during a dinner party at our home. We had all been open with each other about our relationships for some time, so it felt like a natural progression in our friendships. However, when coming out to family members, I chose to have one-on-one conversations rather than a group discussion.

It's also crucial to consider your partner(s) and their feelings when deciding how and where to come out. You may want to discuss the process with them beforehand to ensure you're both on the same page.

Preparing for Different Reactions

You'll likely encounter a range of reactions when coming out as polyamorous, from understanding and acceptance to confusion and judgment. Prepare yourself for these different responses by anticipating common questions and concerns.

Some people may ask about the logistics of your relationships or wonder why you're choosing non-monogamy. Be prepared to explain how polyamory works for you and address any misconceptions they may have. Others might express worry about the impact on your existing relationships or fear that you're "putting yourself out there" too much.

Anticipate these reactions by thinking about common concerns and practicing responses in advance. Remember, it's okay to take time to respond to questions or to say you'll discuss something later when you have more information.
